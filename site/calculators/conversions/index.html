    Conversion calculators

    Convert between metric and imperial units for the things that come up in everyday life: distances, weights, temperatures, volumes, speeds and areas. One tool, one input, all the sensible outputs at once.

    Why unit conversions cause more trouble than they should

    The UK has been officially metric for the best part of fifty years and yet, on any given day, you can be asked your height in feet and inches, your weight in stones, the distance to the next service station in miles, the petrol you're filling up in litres, and the room temperature in Celsius. The cookbook on the shelf might be metric, but the recipe your grandmother wrote out is in cups. Your imported car has a manual in km/L. None of it is going to change soon, and none of it makes mental arithmetic easy.

    The other complication is that some "imperial" units are not the same on both sides of the Atlantic. A US gallon is not a UK gallon. A US tablespoon is not a UK tablespoon. Cup measures vary depending on whose cookbook you're reading. A converter that doesn't tell you which cup it's using is, in baking terms, the difference between a sponge that rises and a sponge that doesn't. The Cooking Measurements Converter separates UK, US and metric on purpose, because the alternative is a ruined batch of biscuits.

    Where the input number really comes from

    Most conversions start with a number someone else gave you: a recipe, a spec sheet, a dosage, a customs form, a sat-nav. The first job is reading what unit it's actually in. American recipes assume US cups even when they don't say so. European car spec sheets quote fuel economy in litres per 100 kilometres, which is the inverse of miles per gallon, so a smaller number is better. Aviation altitudes are in feet, even in countries that are otherwise fully metric. Knowing which convention applies to the source is more than half the battle.

    For everyday measurements, a tape measure or kitchen scale gives you the cleanest input. For things you're estimating, like a room size or a parcel weight, round to a sensible precision before converting. Carrying six decimal places of "approximately a metre and a half" is fake precision, and it tends to look worse, not better, in the converted answer.

    Common mistakes worth avoiding

    The first is mixing dry and liquid measurements. A cup of flour weighs nothing like a cup of water, and converting "1 cup flour" to grams via a generic volume formula gives you nonsense. Use a converter that knows about ingredient density, or weigh the ingredient instead.

    The second is forgetting that temperature conversions are not multiplications. Celsius and Fahrenheit don't share a zero. You can't scale them, you have to translate them. The same goes for time zones, where daylight saving moves the goalposts twice a year.

    The third is precision laundering. If your input is "about three feet", your output is "about a metre". It is not "0.9144 metres". The Unit Converter will give you that level of precision because the maths supports it, but pasting the long decimal into a quote or a contract is how you end up arguing with a contractor about two millimetres that nobody actually measured.

    Frequently asked questions

    Why are US and metric cooking measurements so different?

    The US uses volume (cups, tablespoons) for almost everything. The rest of the world weighs solids in grams and measures liquids in millilitres. Volume measurements depend on how packed the ingredient is (a packed cup of brown sugar weighs more than a loose one), so weight is more reliable. The Cooking Measurements Converter handles both.

    What is the difference between fluid ounces and weight ounces?

    A fluid ounce (fl oz) measures volume; an ounce (oz) measures weight. They are not the same thing and the relationship depends on the substance. One fluid ounce of water weighs about one ounce, but one fluid ounce of olive oil weighs less, and one fluid ounce of honey weighs more. The cooking converter shows both where it matters.

    Why is there no exact conversion for some cooking ingredients?

    Because cup-to-grams depends on density. A cup of flour weighs 120 to 140 grams depending on how it is scooped and packed; a cup of grated cheese can vary by 30 per cent. The converter uses standard average values from professional kitchens, which is good enough for almost any home recipe.

    Are these conversion factors the official ones?

    Yes. Length, weight and volume use the internationally agreed SI definitions (1 inch = 25.4 mm exactly, 1 lb = 453.592 g exactly). Temperature uses the standard °F to °C formula. Cooking ingredient densities use USDA and food-science standard values. Differences from other converters online are usually rounding choices.

    Does any of my data leave the browser?

    No. Every conversion runs in JavaScript on your device. Useful when you are converting client measurements, recipe drafts, or anything else you would rather not paste into a server-side tool.
